1. Helicopter development lagged behind fixed-wing aircraft because helicopters need more power, withstand higher stresses, have more moving parts and are harder to control.
2. Early problems: insufficient power, poor control, severe vibration, high maintenance and structural stress.
3. First practical helicopter: Focke-Wulf Fw-61 (1938).
4. Early contemporaries: Weir W-5 and Sikorsky VS-300.
5. WWII accelerated helicopter R&D and production; German work by Anton Flettner and Heinrich Focke led early advances.
6. Focke-Achgelis Fa-223 crossed the English Channel on 6 Sept 1945.
7. Post-war understanding of dynamics and stability produced safer, easier-to-fly helicopters.
8. First certified helicopter: Bell 47 (1946).
9. First British certified helicopter: Bristol Sycamore (1952) — noted for light control forces (no power assistance).
10. Turbine engines (higher power-to-weight, fewer moving parts) were introduced in the early 1950s, transforming performance and payload capacity.
11. First turbine-powered helicopter flight: Kaman K-225 (1951); first twin-turbine Kaman in 1954.
12. Bell Huey: Bell UH-1 Iroquois first flew in 1956 and typified the turbine era.
13. 1960s marked system-level maturity in helicopter design; durable, upgradeable designs emerged and remain in service.
14. Long-lived designs: Sikorsky S-61 and Boeing CH-47 Chinook.
15. Since the 1960s progress has been incremental refinements (composites, lighter engines, improved transmissions) rather than radical breakthroughs.
16. Composite materials (fiber-impregnated resin layers) reduced weight, increased fatigue life, and damped vibration.
17. Modern mechanical refinements produced lighter engines and transmissions with longer service life.
18. Some components (main gearbox, rotor head) remain single-point catastrophic-failure items.
19. Electronic condition monitoring (HUMS: Health and Usage Monitoring Systems) measures stress and exposure time to predict remaining safe life and improve safety.
20. Typical major components: airframe, fuselage, landing gear, powerplant, transmission, main rotor system, tail (anti-torque) rotor.
21. Airframe materials: metal, wood or composites; composites are lighter and better damped.
22. Fuselage houses cabin, crew, passengers, cargo, engine, transmission, avionics and controls; fuel tanks are placed close to the center of mass to minimize trim changes as fuel is used.
23. Landing gear types: wheels (fixed or retractable), skids (fixed), skis, floats; wheels are heavier and more complex (tires, brakes, oleo struts, hydraulics); retractable wheels reduce drag.
24. Skid gear is lighter, simpler, lower maintenance; skid patent and adoption followed operational experience on soft or uneven terrain.
25. Powerplant: pistons for small helicopters, gas turbines for most larger types; turbine assemblies include compressor, combustion chamber, turbine and accessory gearbox; ~75% of incoming airflow often used for engine cooling.
26. Transmission reduces engine rpm to optimum rotor rpm and supplies tail rotor and accessories; includes main rotor transmission, tail drive, clutch and freewheeling unit.
27. Main rotor system: mast (hollow shaft), hub (attachment point), rotor blades — rotating assembly that generates lift.
28. Tail (anti-torque) rotor counters main-rotor torque and controls yaw.
29. Operational roles where helicopters beat aeroplanes: no runway, confined or uneven landing areas, hovering/transfer operations, amphibious operations, rescue/medevac, offshore refuelling/operations, troop/cargo transport and disaster response.
30. Military use (e.g., Vietnam) emphasized troop insertion/removal, resupply, casualty evacuation and recovery missions.
31. Failure causes: corrosion and stress-corrosion cracking can initiate fractures in landing-gear and pylon/flange areas; inspections must detect corrosion pits, microcracks and branched cracks.
32. Maintenance emphasis: monitor critical items, schedule life-limited components by measured stress/time, prioritize gearbox and rotor-head integrity.
33. Summary takeaway for exam: know timeline (early problems → WWII acceleration → postwar stability → 1950s turbines → 1960s system maturity → modern refinements), key examples (entities above), main components and functions, landing-gear types and evolution, composites and HUMS, and which parts remain catastrophic if they fail.
